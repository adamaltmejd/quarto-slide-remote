Opening

Welcome. Today we're going to walk through the architecture of slide-remote in some detail, then look at three tricky design moments that pushed the boundaries of what a static-deck companion can be expected to do.

First, the protocol. Every paired session is brokered by a single Cloudflare Durable Object so there is exactly one authoritative source of truth for slide state per room. Reconnects pick up the latest snapshot rather than replaying events.

Why a Durable Object

We considered three alternatives before settling on DO: a relay-only Worker with no state (rejected — clients had to re-derive everything on join), a Pub/Sub bus over Workers KV (rejected — write latency too high), and a tiny Postgres instance (rejected — operational overhead for what is fundamentally an ephemeral session).

The DO model gives us strong consistency and free rate limiting per room, at the cost of having to think carefully about hibernation. The trickiest moment is the cold-resume: when a DO wakes up after eviction, the WebSocket clients have already buffered messages and are waiting for an ack. We hold all writes until the snapshot is restored from storage; on the order of 50–120 ms in practice.

Three tricky moments

  1. Pair-code collision. Six characters of Crockford base32 gives ~1B codes; collisions only matter across concurrent rooms, but we still rotate every 15 minutes to limit the brute-force window.
  2. Reveal vs. our keydown handler. Reveal binds Esc on document; we needed a capture-phase listener so closing the QR overlay didn't also pop the deck into overview mode. v0.5 widened that to swallow every keydown while the overlay is open.
  3. iOS swipe-back fight. The very first edge-swipe iteration tried to claim x≈0 and lost every time to Safari's system gesture. The fix turned out to be a single CSS line: touch-action: pan-y on body.

Closing remarks

The principle running through all three of these is the same: a static-deck companion lives inside a tightly-bounded browser environment, so the cheapest fix is almost always to respect the platform's expectations rather than fight them. The DO buys us consistency; capture-phase listeners buy us priority; touch-action buys us the gesture. Each is a one-line concession that ends up doing all the work.
